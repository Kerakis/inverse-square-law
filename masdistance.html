The mAs-Distance Compensation Formula

X-rays are a form of electromagnetic radiation, which can be quantified by the number of photons. As these photons are emitted from a point-source origin, they spread out. This can be visualized with visibile light by turning on a flashlight in a dark room. The beam of light spreads out much like how an x-ray beam will spread out from an x-ray tube assembly. As the distance between the radiation source and image receptor is increased, the mAs must be increased to compensate for fewer x-ray photons striking the image receptor in order to maintain the same exposure.

Solving for distance uses the formula:

$$ D_2=\sqrt{mAs_2\cdot D_1^2\over mAs_1} $$

Solving for mAs uses the formula:

$$ mAs_2 = \frac{mAs_1\cdot D_2^2}{D_1^2} $$

\( mAs_1 = \) The original mAs

\( D_1 = \) The original source to image-receptor distance

\( mAs_2 = \) The new mAs

\( D_2 = \) The new source to image-receptor distance
